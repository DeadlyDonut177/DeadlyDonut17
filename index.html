Project Idea

I thought for a long time about what exactly would improve not only my life, but other’s lives, and would do it in a cheap, and logistically possible manner, such that everyone may have access to and benefit from it. My idea is quite simple. Using a sort of person-detection system, along with a stepper motor and an on-board computer, create an air conditioner or fan that consistently directs temperature-regulated air to the location of where a person is, or people are in a room.

Coming from South Africa, and now living in Australia, I understand very well the tiring pain of being uncomfortable from sitting in a room too hot or cold. Now that we are entering the summer, I think most Australians would agree with me that innovation in temperature regulation is needed right now. At least in our first world country, we have the technological ability to simply blow air to wherever we’re sitting, and yet here we are, lacking an auto air conditioner. I am not impressed by our current global air conditioners that heat or cool an entire building, but a localised heating and cooling solution will be more energy efficient than any alternative.

For simplicity, from here on we’ll call this product AutoCooler.
AutoCooler will detect where a person is in a room, then will rotate a typical fan toward the direction of where that person is. This breaks down and may become confusing when multiple people are in a room, but some simple software adjustments can account for this, which I will explain later. AutoCooler consists of two independent heads. Both heads are operated by a two axis gimbal. The first head holds the fan or could hold an air conditioning unit duct, while the second head holds the person detector sensor. Again for simplicity, we’ll rename the first head to the ‘fan’ and the second head to the ‘sensor’. Both the fan and the sensor are operated by an on-board microcontroller, and physically moved by two stepper motors. Initially when starting the AutoCoolerer, the fan will direct air in whichever direction it is facing. Immediately upon startup however, the sensor will begin scanning. The sensor will begin to slowly rotate and nod up and down until the entire room has been scanned. Detecting the physically warmest spots in the room, the sensor sends this data to the microcontroller, which in turns commands the fan to rotate and nod to meet that particular position. The sensor never turns off, as long as the AutoCoolerer is still on. This way, the fan is always pointed in the direction of a person in the room. A modified AutoCoolerer uses multiple sensors attached to the same sensor head, each facing a different direction. This way, more information can be gathered faster, allowing for faster and more accurate updates sent to the fan. A problem some may have thought of is the issue of having multiple people in the same room. This issue is edited by software in the microcontroller, and can be adjusted by the user as they choose. If the sensor or sensors detect multiple separate hotspots, the fan head will focus on one hotspot for a decided amount of time, before alternating to a different hotspot. By adjusting a knob on the AutoCoolerer, the user can dictate the length of this set time. A manual mode is also available, by pressing the mode button, thus switching modes, and by momentarily pressing on 1 or multiple of 4 buttons on the AutoCoolerer, the fan head can be rotated to point in the specific direction of the user’s choosing. The sensor will continue to scan, to allow for instantaneous return to automatic mode, without the delay of rescanning the room. A third mode is available. Possibly for secret use on a roommate or girlfriend, the microcontroller is reprogrammed to direct the fan head in the exact opposite direction of a person in a room. With these modes, a simple technological improvement is brought to the world, adding to the great rise in automation, and to the possibilities of ways to annoy housemates.

For the manufacturing of this product, a base can be either injection molded or 3D printed out of plastic, alternatively other materials of bases can be used. Both the sensor and fan head can be rotated 360 degrees using a stepper motor, while the nodding function for Y-Axis control can be filled by using a simple servo. The fan head makes use of an ordinary desktop fan, but design iterations may allow for air conditioning ducts to be used. The sensor head is more tricky, but after some interesting deliberation, it is decided that a single pixel IR camera would be a lightweight, cheap, simple and fulfilling heat sensor.

A large amount of skill is required during the design process, while the operation, and large scale production of this device will not be skill demanding. A mount for the build must be first 3D modelled, which will require knowledge of 3D modelling as well as knowledge about the manufacturing process. All physical parts, besides the custom base, can be sourced from local appropriate suppliers. The software will take a large amount of skill. A semi-complex algorithm will be programmed, to notice clusters of “warm” pixels, and these must then be assigned as being single people, or groups of people, and take user input to determine the length of time the fan must then focus on these spots. Additionally, a simple algorithm must be coded to determine the difference between the temperature of empty spaces, and people.

If this product can be manufactured, shipped and sold for a low price, these could become as commercialised as regular rotating desktop fans. People will cease to have to worry about feeling comfortable while working or relaxing. The guilt of paying large bills or using inefficient full building coolers and heaters will also be eliminated. With this I believe firmly, that with better relaxed, and more efficient working people, our economy and society as a whole will work better, faster and happier.
